Music Player UX · Mobile Product Redesign

Resonance makes listening feel more immersive, expressive, and alive.

A detailed Spotify redesign focused on the now-playing experience, adaptive visuals, layered context, gesture onboarding, and micro-interactions that make music feel less static.

Overview

Reframing the player as an experience, not just a control screen.

The opportunity

Most music players do the functional job well, but the emotional side of listening often feels underused. This redesign explores how Spotify could turn the now-playing area into a richer, more ambient experience without making core actions harder to reach.

Problem Standard player layouts can feel flat, repetitive, and disconnected from the mood of the music.
Goal Make listening feel more personal through motion, adaptive theming, lyrics, and contextual layers.
Outcome A concept-driven case study with focused improvements around delight, immersion, and depth.

What this redesign concentrates on

Instead of redesigning the entire Spotify ecosystem, the work stays intentionally centered on the moment when a user is already playing music and wants deeper engagement.

Now playing A clearer hierarchy with stronger album art presence and more intentional controls.
Lyrics overlay A dedicated reading layer that keeps the track feeling present while making lyrics easier to follow.
Context panels Expandable spaces for additional music context without overloading the default screen.

Project Brief

Designing a player that reacts to what the listener feels.

Challenge
Create a music-player experience that feels more expressive and emotionally connected without sacrificing clarity.
Audience
Mobile listeners who value atmosphere, quick control, and richer engagement during playback.
Scope
Entry state, now-playing screen, lyrics mode, context panels, gesture onboarding, theming, and interaction detail.
Design Move
Use depth, motion, visual adaptation, and layered information to make the player feel dynamic rather than static.

Design Principles

Three ideas shaped the visual and interaction direction.

Music should set the mood of the UI

Adaptive theming helps the screen feel like it belongs to the current track rather than staying visually generic.

More depth, not more clutter

Extra information lives in layers and panels so the main player stays calm while power features stay available.

Delight comes from motion and response

Small moments like onboarding and like-state animation make the interface feel tactile and rewarding.

Experience Flow

A walkthrough of the listening journey across the redesign.

02

03

04

Feature Highlights

The most distinctive moves in the concept.

Dynamic theming from album art

Color becomes part of the storytelling. The player adapts to artwork so the experience feels track-specific and less repetitive across sessions.

Gesture onboarding for discoverability

Helpful onboarding reduces the learning curve for layered interactions, making the richer experience easier to understand from the start.

Context panels that add depth

Additional panels suggest a more expandable player ecosystem where supporting information can surface without crowding the default interface.

Like animation for emotional feedback

Saving a song feels more satisfying when the state change is communicated as a moment, not just a small icon flip.

User Interface

Typography, colour, grid, and prototype direction.

Typography

A dark cinematic shell with adaptive accents

The interface uses a strong display hierarchy, compact metadata text, and bright playback accents so the now-playing moment feels premium, focused, and responsive.

Grid

4-column mobile grid with 24px margins and 16px gutters

The layout is built on a centered 390px mobile frame using a four-column stretch grid, giving the player enough rhythm for album art, metadata, controls, and expandable panels.
